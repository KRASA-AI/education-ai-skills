# Formative Assessment & Differentiation AI Tools — Landscape (2026)

Snapshot of the current ecosystem of AI tools that educators use for rapid formative assessment, text leveling, behavior support, and family communication. Use this as background when running the related skills so output recommendations are grounded in real platforms teachers may already use.

## Exit Tickets & Rapid Formative Assessment

Exit tickets are 1–5 item checks for understanding administered in the final 3–5 minutes of class. AI generators reduce creation time from ~15 minutes to under 2 minutes, which is the threshold at which teachers shift from occasional to daily use.

Notable platforms: TeachQuill, SchoolAI, OpenEduCat, EasyClass, Weavely, plus broader suites (MagicSchool, Brisk, Eduaide). Typical features include objective alignment, Bloom's level selection, misconception-driven distractors, and Google Forms export.

Formats commonly supported: multiple choice, short answer, 3-2-1 reflection, muddiest point, confidence scaling. The 3-2-1 format (3 things learned, 2 questions, 1 application) generalizes well across subjects.

## Text Leveling & Reading Accessibility

Tools that rewrite passages up or down a reading level while preserving meaning. Manual rewrites take 45–90 minutes per 1,000-word passage; AI tools do this in seconds. Target levels are typically specified as Lexile number, grade band, or ELL proficiency tier (Beginner / Intermediate / Advanced).

Notable platforms: Brisk Teaching (Change Reading Level), Diffit, CK-12 Reading Level Adjuster, Flint K12, Knowt, OpenEduCat, Blueye, CoGrader. Key capability: preserving tier-3 content vocabulary while simplifying tier-2 words and syntax.

Primary use cases: mixed-ability reading groups, ELL accommodation, access to grade-level content for striving readers, and stretching advanced readers with elevated versions of the same text.

## Behavior Intervention & Special Education Supports

AI adoption in special education surged in 2024–25: roughly 60% of special education teachers reported using AI on IEPs or 504 plans, up ~18 points year-over-year. Most common uses: summarizing IEPs, selecting accommodations, and drafting narrative portions.

Notable platforms: MagicSchool AI (accommodation suggester, BIP templates), Playground IEP (goal and accommodation generation from present-level statements), CK-12 IEP Planner.

Risks flagged repeatedly in the 2025–26 press: student privacy (PII in unapproved tools), bias in generated accommodations, and the risk that AI drafts substitute for the legally required FBA/IEP team process. Best practice: treat AI output as a team-meeting starting point, not a final plan; redact PII before input; confirm district-approved tooling; maintain team, parent, and (age-appropriate) student voice.

BIP-specific capabilities emerging in platforms: antecedent strategies, function-based replacement behaviors, reinforcement plans, de-escalation steps, and data collection templates. No platform yet replaces a qualified behavior specialist for plans subject to IDEA.

## Family Communication & Classroom Newsletters

Newsletter generators produce weekly/monthly family updates from teacher bullet notes. Time saved: ~30 minutes per newsletter. Translation support is increasingly standard.

Notable platforms: Khanmigo (Create a Class Newsletter, free in the U.S.), Brisk Newsletter Generator, TeachQuill, Knowt, CK-12 Class Newsletter Creator, OpenEduCat.

Best-practice patterns for family-facing writing: 6th–8th grade Lexile regardless of class grade level; strip acronyms (RTI, MTSS, SEL) or define inline; lead with 2–3 concrete "Ways to Support at Home"; keep subject lines under ~55 characters; provide translated versions as separate documents rather than interleaving languages.

## Cross-Cutting Market Signals (2026)

- 68% of educators report AI saves them up to 5 hours/week; 58% use AI primarily as a productivity tool
- AI usage in classrooms has increased roughly 6× since 2023
- 65% of educators say AI helps bridge resource gaps — platform fatigue and lack of integration are the dominant headwinds
- Curriculum Intelligence (Brisk) and agent-style assistants (OpenClaw, Taskade) are the emerging differentiators vs. single-purpose tools
- District guidance increasingly emphasizes "AI drafts, teachers finalize" as the default operating model

## Practitioner Heuristic

Across all four tool categories the pattern is consistent: AI handles the first-draft, repetitive work; the educator retains professional judgment, cultural context, and relationship. Skills in this repo should default to outputs labeled as drafts, require teacher review before student- or family-facing use, and never pretend to replace the human judgment the legal and pedagogical frameworks assume.
