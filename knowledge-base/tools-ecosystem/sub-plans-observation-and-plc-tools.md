# Sub Plans, Classroom Observation, Restorative, and PLC AI Tools — Landscape (2026)

Snapshot of the AI-tool ecosystem supporting four distinct instructional-leadership and teacher-productivity workflows. Use this as background when running the related skills so output recommendations are grounded in real platforms educators may already use.

## Substitute Lesson Plan Generators

Sub plans are one of the most time-expensive non-teaching tasks on a teacher's plate — a thorough set for a full day typically took 45–90 minutes before AI entered the workflow. AI sub-plan generators reduce this to 3–10 minutes for pre-planned absences and under 5 minutes for emergency "standing sub plans" that every teacher should have on file from the first week of school.

Notable platforms: SubPlan.ai (Swing Education), OpenEduCat Substitute Lesson Plan Generator, Knowt Substitute Plan AI Tool, SchoolGPT Sub Plan Generator, MagicSchool AI (sub-plan template in the free educator suite). Typical capabilities include a time-stamped activity sequence, an independent student task that does not require content expertise from the sub, classroom-management scripts, and a structured note-back form.

Best-practice pattern: the plan must be readable by a stranger. Every direction the sub is expected to say aloud should appear in quotes so the sub can read it verbatim; every teacher acronym (CFU, WICOR, Tier 2) must be spelled out; and every material referenced must be paired with a physical location in the room ("packets are in the blue tray on my desk"). Emergency plans bias toward universally-valuable independent work — vocabulary review, reading with comprehension, writing prompts with graphic organizers — not movies or free periods.

## Classroom Observation & Teacher-Coaching Feedback Tools

Classroom observation moved from paper checklists to digital platforms over the last decade; AI is now a layer on top that auto-tags instructional practices, transcribes lessons, maps evidence to rubric indicators, and drafts post-observation feedback. As of 2026 these tools are moving from "experimental extras" into core coaching infrastructure for districts running instructional-coaching cycles at scale.

Notable platforms: Observation Copilot, 2gno.me, Edthena AI Coach, EducationWalkthrough, Kyron Learning (TeachFX), and framework-aligned modules inside broader LMS suites. Framework alignment is non-negotiable — most districts operate on Danielson 4-domain (2022 revision), Marzano Focused Teacher Evaluation, NIET TAP, or a state-specific model (Texas T-TESS, Florida FEAPs, NY APPR), and tools that force a one-size-fits-all rubric get poor adoption.

Research signal: automated NLP-based feedback on teachers' questioning quality has shown measurable improvements in classroom discourse without replacing human coaching. The dominant 2026 operating model is "human → AI → human": observer captures evidence, AI structures and drafts feedback, observer reviews and finalizes before the post-observation conference. Tools that position AI as the sole feedback channel have not held up against coaching research that emphasizes the human conversation.

Best-practice pattern: evidence-before-claim (no rubric claim without a time-stamped piece of evidence); narrow growth (1–2 high-leverage moves per cycle, not a laundry list); stage-match (walkthroughs cover 1–2 indicators, full observations may cover more); and coaching-first register in formative cycles, evaluative register reserved for summative cycles with explicit framing.

## Restorative Practices & Conference Facilitation Supports

AI in restorative practice is the newest and most cautious of these four categories. The international practice base — IIRP Real Justice, CRJ, community-specific traditions such as Navajo peacemaking and Māori whānau conferencing — depends on trained facilitators, voluntary participation, and careful sequencing that AI is not positioned to replace. What AI can reasonably do is draft a facilitator-facing script that scaffolds the preamble, the restorative questions, the agreement-to-repair template, and the follow-up checkpoints.

Notable patterns: no widely-adopted standalone "restorative AI platform" has emerged as of 2026; the practice lives primarily inside LMS-integrated SEL and behavior-support suites (e.g., Kickboard, PBIS Rewards) and district-built templates. The 2026 field is still distinctly human.

Non-negotiable safeguards: voluntariness (explicit consent from every party before any joint meeting); mandated-reporter boundary (if the conference surfaces abuse, neglect, or credible safety threat, the facilitator pauses and follows reporting protocols — restorative practice does not supersede mandated reporting); appropriate scope (incidents involving weapons, sexual harm, or Title IX do not go to restorative conference alone); language access (qualified interpreters, not peers or family members when minors are involved); and trauma-informed pacing (pauses, regulation supports, pass options for every question). Outputs should always be labeled as drafts for trained facilitators, never as turn-key scripts for untrained staff.

## Professional Learning Community Agenda & Data-Protocol Tools

PLCs have been the dominant collaborative-teacher structure in U.S. K–12 since DuFour's Learning by Doing (2010); the four DuFour questions (what do we want students to learn; how will we know they learned it; what will we do if they didn't; what will we do if they did) remain the operating frame. AI agenda generators reduce the facilitator-prep burden from 20–45 minutes per meeting to under 5, while preserving the core protocol structure.

Notable platforms: Knowt PLC Agenda Generator, Edthena AI Coach (between-meeting record of coaching cycles that feeds PLC conversations), MagicSchool AI meeting-agenda templates, and district-built protocol libraries on top of Google Docs or Notion. Protocol sources in wide use: Solution Tree PLC at Work, Data Wise improvement process (Harvard), NSRF Critical Friends protocols, LASW (Looking at Student Work), and Collaborative Assessment Conference.

Best-practice pattern: protocol must fit the data on the table. Common formative data calls for LASW or data-driven dialogue. Interim-assessment data fits Data Wise Steps 3–5. Behavior data fits Check-In/Check-Out review. Survey data fits Tuning. Mismatched protocols are the first cause of PLC drift; over-packed agendas are the second; mixing observation / interpretation / action inside one block is the third. Every commitment leaving the room must have a named owner, a target date, and a visible-evidence metric, or it will not transfer to next week's instruction.

## Cross-Cutting Market Signals (2026)

- Platform fatigue is the dominant 2026 headwind across all four categories — educators manage an average of eight digital tools, and 50% report feeling overwhelmed by the number of platforms
- Framework alignment is the acceptance test for leadership-facing tools (observation, PLC): if it doesn't speak the district's rubric or protocol language, it doesn't get adopted
- "Human → AI → human" is the prevailing operating model for sensitive workflows (observation feedback, restorative scripts, sub plans for classes with IEPs); tools that route around the human in the middle underperform in longitudinal studies
- Federal grant priorities finalized in April 2026 elevate "AI to reduce administrative tasks" as a named priority, which is directly where sub plans, observation feedback, and PLC agendas sit

## Practitioner Heuristic

Across all four tool categories the pattern is consistent: AI handles the structure (time-stamped agendas, rubric-aligned evidence tables, question sequences, commitment grids); the trained educator retains the judgment, the relationship, and the authority. Skills in this repo should default to outputs labeled as drafts for a trained professional, should scope themselves explicitly to what the AI can defensibly generate from the inputs provided, and should never position AI as a replacement for the trained facilitator, coach, or substitute the legal and pedagogical frameworks assume.
